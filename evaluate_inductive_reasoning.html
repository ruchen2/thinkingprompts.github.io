Evalute Inductive Reasoning

  • Evaluate generalization
    • Was the correct group sampled?
    • Were the data obtained in an effective way?
    • Were enough cases considered?
    • Was the sample representatively structured?
  • Distinguish coincidences, correlations, and causes
  • Fallacies disguising as strong inductive arguments
    • Erroneous generalization: generalizations, even those based on solid evidence, can be deceptively fallacious, too (for example, exaggeration)
    • Playing with numbers: selectely use of raw numbers or percentages, provide numbers without sufficient information
    • False dilemma (the either/or fallacy): more options emerge on further examination
    • The gambler's fallacy: wrongly assume that what happened by chance is somehow correlated or causally connected with things we can control
    • False cause
      • Confuse temporal proximity with causuality: since event Y followed event X, event Y must have been caused by event X
      • Confuse a correlation with cause
      • Confuse symptons, outcomes, or intentions with causes
    • Slippery Slope
    • Wrongly assume that discrete events are linked together so that the first step in the process necessarily results in some significant, usually bad, consequences way down the road somewhere
